The Royal Library of Ashurbanipal

Founding

Named after its founder King Ashurbanipal, the Library of Ashurbanipal was a collection of at least 30,000 clay cuneiform tablets. It was founded in the 7th century BCE, in Nineveh, Mesopotamia (now known as Iraq). These tablets are believed to be King Ashurbanipal’s royal library.

What was in the Library?

The library’s collections included scholarly and academic texts, history, documents and records, and literature. The Ashurbanipal Library Project describes it as “the largest, broadest and most important library ever assembled over 3,500 years of cuneiform culture.”

Did this one burn down?

It did…but the tablets survived! The Neo-Assyrian Empire fell to Babylonians, Medes, and Persians, who burnt the library down. But, due to the “books” in this case being made of clay, the fire only hardened and strengthened them, preserving them for thousands and thousands of years. In fact, some can still be read on this website today!
