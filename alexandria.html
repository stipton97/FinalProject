The Library of Alexandria

Founding

Probably the most famous ancient library in the world, the Library of Alexandria was founded in 295 BCE. The library’s founding is attributed to disgraced Greek politician Demetrius of Phaleron, who came to Alexandria after being exiled, and the successor to Alexander the Great, Ptolemy I Soter.

What was in the Library?

The Library of Alexandria had over 700,000 scrolls, written on parchment. Since the idea of the Library was to document and store as much human knowledge as possible, these covered many subjects, from poetry to medicine.

So…did it burn down?

Well…yes and no. Everything from movies to EPCOT rides depicts it this way, but the loss of the Library was actually a long process that had to do with politics just as much as it did fire. After Ptolemy VIII rose to power, he expelled or killed many Alexandrian scholars and intellectuals, leaving the library in a dire state. By the time Caesar accidentally set part of it aflame during a war in 48 BCE, it had been in a slow tailspin for nearly a century.
